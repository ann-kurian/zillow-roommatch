Product Overview

What Zillow does — and where it falls short

Zillow is the United States' leading housing marketplace — used by renters, buyers, and sellers to browse listings, access price estimates, and explore neighbourhood data. In San Francisco specifically, where median one-bedroom rents exceed $2,800 per month, the platform is the dominant starting point for apartment hunting. Its rental search feature offers map-based browsing, detailed filters, virtual tours, and comprehensive listing information.

For most renters the experience is well-designed. But for students and early-career professionals in a city where shared living is not a preference but a financial necessity — Zillow stops short of solving the full problem. It helps users find a place to live. It does not help them find someone to live with.

Problem Statement
"Zillow does not provide a one-stop solution for users seeking rentals with an intention to share with roommates — forcing them to manage two separate searches across two different platforms."

SWOT Analysis

Where Zillow leads — and where the opportunity lies

Strengths
Extensive, frequently updated property database with national coverage
Powerful, precise search filters returning personalised results
Detailed listings with photos, virtual tours, and full feature descriptions
Interactive map-based browsing with neighbourhood context
Strong brand recognition and trust in the US real estate market
Weaknesses
No roommate finder — users seeking a co-tenant must leave the platform
Recommendation engine does not account for past search behaviour
No commute time or nearby amenity proximity search from a listing
Opportunities
Launch a roommate matching feature for students and young professionals in high-cost cities like San Francisco
Expand into an end-to-end platform covering applications, digital lease signing, and rent payments
Threats
Competition from Apartments.com, Trulia, Craigslist, and Facebook Marketplace
SF housing market instability creating unpredictable shifts in user behaviour
Varying California rental regulations and evolving tenant protections

SF Neighbourhood + Commute Tags
Profile setup uses real SF neighbourhoods (Marina, Nob Hill, Mission, SOMA, Hayes Valley) and SF-specific commute tags (Near BART, Near Caltrain, WFH friendly). These tags directly reflect how SF renters think about location trade-offs.
4
Budget + Commute Visible on Cards
Match cards show monthly budget share and a commute tag alongside lifestyle preferences. In SF's market, these two factors are the primary filters — a $1,300/mo share in the Mission looks very different from $1,800/mo in the Marina.
5
Split Rent Calculator
The calculator pulls the listing price from the user's saved property and shows the real 50/50 split including utilities. It also flags when a match's stated budget is close but slightly below — giving users the information to decide whether the gap is negotiable before they message.
6
Calculator Shared in Chat
The split rent summary is shareable within the conversation — so both parties are looking at the same numbers when discussing a specific listing. This removes a common friction point: miscommunication about actual costs before a viewing is even booked.

Why Build This?

Value for both sides

RoomMatch is particularly compelling in San Francisco because the problem is especially acute — median rents are among the highest in the country, shared living is the dominant rental pattern for the under-35 cohort, and the current tools for finding a roommate (Craigslist, Facebook groups) are fragmented and high-friction.

For Users
Eliminates the need to manage two separate searches — Zillow for listings, Craigslist or Facebook for roommates
Reduces effective rent by 40–55% through cost-sharing — critical in a city where solo rents exceed $2,800/mo for a one-bedroom
Split rent calculator shows the real cost before any conversation begins — removing a common source of miscommunication
SF-specific commute tags (BART, Caltrain) surface the proximity trade-offs that matter most to this market
In-app messaging removes the risk of sharing personal contact details before trust is established
For Zillow
Increases session depth and time-in-app by adding a social engagement layer to a search-only product
Creates new monetisation pathways — premium verified profiles, background check integrations, move-in concierge
Improves retention by making Zillow the complete rental platform rather than just a discovery tool
SF as a launch market provides a concentrated, high-intent user base — ideal for testing before national rollout
Clear competitive differentiation — no equivalent integrated feature on Apartments.com, Trulia, or Craigslist
Success Metrics

How we would measure success

Metric 01 — Activation
% of SF rental searches with RoomMatch enabled
Feature adoption rate among the SF target cohort — tracks how many users activate RoomMatch during an active SF rental search session.
Metric 02 — Outcome
Number of successful roommate matches per month
A match is defined as a mutual connection request followed by at least one in-app message exchange. SF launch target: 500 matches in Month 1.
Metric 03 — Feature Adoption
% of match detail views with split rent calculator used
Specific to the SF MVP — tracks whether users are engaging with the calculator as a decision-making tool, not just scrolling past it.
Metric 04 — Retention
User retention rate among RoomMatch users vs. non-users
Measures whether RoomMatch creates platform stickiness — do users who engage with it continue using Zillow after finding a roommate?
Metric 05 — Growth
% increase in new SF users attributable to RoomMatch
Tracks whether RoomMatch attracts net-new users who were previously using Craigslist or Facebook for their SF roommate search.
North Star Metric
Users who save a San Francisco listing and achieve a RoomMatch connection in the same session
The single metric that captures RoomMatch's core value — a user who finds both a property and a potential roommate in one session, without leaving Zillow. This is the behaviour that proves the feature is delivering on its premise in the SF market.
Reflection

What the analysis reveals

Framing this proposal specifically around San Francisco changes the nature of the argument. In a generic US rental market, shared living is a preference. In San Francisco, it is a financial necessity for most renters under 30. That distinction makes the split rent calculator not a nice-to-have feature but a core part of the decision workflow — and it makes the BART and Caltrain commute tags directly relevant rather than decorative.

San Francisco also makes sense as a launch market for a different reason: the user density is high, the problem is acute, and the cohort is tech-literate — meaning adoption friction is lower than it would be in a mid-tier market. A successful SF launch would generate the data needed to refine the matching algorithm before a national rollout.

"In San Francisco, the question is never whether you need a roommate. The question is how you find one you can trust, at a rent you can both afford, near the train you both need. That is a product problem — and Zillow is the right platform to solve it."

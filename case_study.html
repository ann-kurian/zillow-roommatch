Product Overview

What Zillow does — and where it falls short

Zillow is the United States' leading housing marketplace — used by renters, buyers, and sellers to browse listings, access price estimates, and explore neighbourhood data. Its rental search feature offers map-based browsing, detailed filters, virtual tours, and comprehensive listing information, making it the dominant starting point for apartment hunting across the country.

For most renters the experience is well-designed and intuitive. But for one specific and growing cohort — students and early-career professionals seeking to share a rental — Zillow's product stops short of solving the full problem. It helps users find a place to live. It does not help them find someone to live with.

Problem Statement
"Zillow does not provide a one-stop solution for users seeking rentals with an intention to share with roommates — forcing them to manage two separate searches across two different platforms."
Existing Feature Analysis

The current rental search user flow

The existing Zillow rental search flow is well-structured up to the point of listing selection. The gap becomes apparent at the filter stage — where preferences can be refined by price, bedrooms, and amenities, but not by whether a co-tenant is being sought.

01
Sign In
User authenticates via Google, Apple, or email. Standard onboarding with low friction.
02
Location Search
User searches by city, neighbourhood, or ZIP. Autocomplete and map integration make this fast and accurate.
03
Results — List View
Relevant listings returned in a scrollable list with price, bedrooms, distance, and preview photos.
04
Results — Map View
Optional toggle to map view showing listings as pins, useful for proximity and neighbourhood-based decision making.
05
Listing Selection
User opens a listing to view full details — photos, floor plans, virtual tours, amenities, and contact information.
06
Filter Application — the gap begins here
Users can refine by price, bedrooms, and amenities, but there is no filter or pathway for users who want to find a co-tenant. They are forced off the platform to Craigslist or Facebook to complete their search.
SWOT Analysis

Where Zillow leads — and where the opportunity lies

Strengths
Extensive, frequently updated property database with national coverage
Powerful, precise search filters returning personalised results
Detailed listings with photos, virtual tours, and full feature descriptions
Interactive map-based browsing with neighbourhood context
Strong brand recognition and trust in the US real estate market
Weaknesses
No roommate finder — users seeking a co-tenant must go to Craigslist or Facebook
Recommendation engine does not account for past search behaviour
No commute time or nearby amenity proximity search from a specific listing
Opportunities
Launch a roommate matching feature targeting students and young professionals in high-cost cities
Expand into an end-to-end platform covering applications, digital lease signing, rent payments, and maintenance
Threats
Competition from Apartments.com, Trulia, Craigslist, and Facebook Marketplace
Housing market instability creating unpredictable shifts in user behaviour
Varying rental regulations and policies across US states

1
Contextual Discovery
RoomMatch is introduced as a banner within Zillow's existing search flow — not a separate app. The feature appears naturally when a user is already browsing rentals, dramatically reducing the barrier to activation.
2
Clear Value Before Opt-in
The enable screen communicates three specific benefits upfront: preference matching, in-app messaging, and shared search. Users understand exactly what they are opting into before sharing any data — building trust from the start.
3
Low-friction Profile Setup
Chip-based selection with sensible defaults. Budget and move-in date are structured inputs; lifestyle tags are tap-to-toggle. No text entry required. The complete setup takes under 90 seconds — critical for a student or time-poor professional audience.
4
Relevance-ranked Matches
Matches are ranked by a compatibility score calculated from overlapping budget, lifestyle preferences, move-in date, and target neighbourhoods. Only users searching in the same areas appear — keeping the feed highly relevant.
5
Shared Listing Visibility
The match detail screen surfaces listings the other user has also saved — creating an immediate, concrete basis for a conversation. Users see overlapping intent before they message, reducing low-quality or mismatched connections.
6
End-to-end Within Zillow
In-app messaging completes the loop. Users can coordinate viewings, share listings, and agree on terms without leaving Zillow. This is the north star: one platform for the full rental decision — where to live and who to live with.

Why Build This?

Value for both sides

A feature only makes strategic sense if it creates clear, measurable value for users and a defensible advantage for the product. RoomMatch does both — solving a genuine, unmet user need while simultaneously improving the engagement, retention, and monetisation metrics that matter most to Zillow.

For Users
Eliminates the need to manage two separate searches — one on Zillow for listings, one on Craigslist or Facebook for roommates
Reduces effective rent through cost-sharing — directly addresses the primary financial constraint of the target cohort
Preference matching reduces time spent on incompatible conversations
In-app messaging removes the risk of sharing personal contact details before trust is established
Especially valuable for users relocating from out of state or internationally who have no existing local network
For Zillow
Increases session depth and time-in-app by adding a social engagement layer to a search-only product
Creates new monetisation pathways — premium matching tiers, verified profiles, background check integrations
Improves retention by making Zillow the complete rental platform rather than just a discovery tool
Expands the addressable user base to users who were not previously searching for their own unit
Clear competitive differentiation — no equivalent integrated feature exists on Apartments.com, Trulia, or Craigslist
Success Metrics

How we would measure success

Metric 01 — Activation
% of rental searches with RoomMatch enabled
Feature adoption rate — tracks how many users in the target cohort activate RoomMatch during an active rental search session.
Metric 02 — Outcome
Number of successful roommate matches per month
A match is defined as a mutual connection request followed by at least one in-app message exchange between the two users.
Metric 03 — Quality
Average time to find a match
Tracks matching algorithm quality. Shorter time-to-match indicates better preference alignment and reduced search friction for users.
Metric 04 — Retention
User retention rate among RoomMatch users
Measures whether users who engage with RoomMatch continue using Zillow after finding a roommate — the long-term platform stickiness signal.
Metric 05 — Growth
% increase in new users via RoomMatch
Tracks whether RoomMatch attracts net-new users to Zillow who were previously using competing platforms for roommate discovery.
North Star Metric
Users who save a listing and achieve a RoomMatch connection in the same session
The single metric that captures RoomMatch's core value proposition — a user who finds both a property and a potential roommate without leaving Zillow. This is the behaviour that proves the feature is working as intended.
Reflection

What the analysis reveals

This teardown started from a straightforward observation: Zillow is highly effective at helping people find where to live, but the platform stops short of the complete problem. For students and young professionals, the decision of where to live and who to live with are not separate — they are made simultaneously, constrained by the same budget. Zillow solves only half of it.

The RoomMatch proposal emerged directly from the SWOT analysis rather than being a preconceived idea. Identifying the absence of a roommate finder as the most structurally significant and competitively addressable weakness — and cross-referencing it with the opportunity space for integrated platform features — is where the feature concept originated. The personas then grounded the proposal in specific, realistic user behaviour, ensuring the feature was designed around a real cohort rather than an abstract need.

"The best feature proposals do not solve hypothetical problems — they solve the friction that users are already working around. Zillow's users do not stop needing a roommate just because Zillow does not offer one. They go to Craigslist instead."

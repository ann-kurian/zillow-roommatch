Product Overview

What Zillow does — and where it falls short

Zillow is the United States' leading housing marketplace used by renters, buyers, and sellers to browse listings, access price estimates, and explore neighbourhood data. In San Francisco specifically, where median one-bedroom rents exceed $2,800 per month, the platform is the dominant starting point for apartment hunting.

For most renters the experience is well-designed. But for students and early-career professionals in a city where shared living is a financial necessity — Zillow stops short. It helps users find a place to live. It does not help them find someone to live with.

Problem Statement
"Zillow does not provide a one-stop solution for users seeking rentals with an intention to share with roommates — forcing them to manage two separate searches across two different platforms."

SWOT Analysis

Where Zillow leads — and where the opportunity lies

Strengths
Extensive, frequently updated property database with national coverage
Powerful filters returning personalised results
Detailed listings with photos, virtual tours, and full descriptions
Interactive map-based browsing with neighbourhood context
Strong brand recognition and trust in the US real estate market
Weaknesses
No roommate finder — users seeking a co-tenant must leave the platform
Recommendation engine does not account for past search behaviour
No commute time or nearby amenity proximity search from a listing
Opportunities
Launch a roommate matching feature for students and young professionals in high-cost cities like San Francisco
Expand into an end-to-end platform covering applications, digital lease signing, and rent payments
Threats
Competition from Apartments.com, Trulia, Craigslist, and Facebook Marketplace
SF housing market instability creating unpredictable shifts in user behaviour
Varying California rental regulations and evolving tenant protections

Why Build This?

Value for both sides

For Users
Eliminates two separate searches — Zillow for listings, Craigslist for roommates
Reduces effective rent by 40–55% — critical where solo rents exceed $2,800/mo
Split rent calculator shows the real cost before any conversation begins
SF commute tags (BART, Caltrain) surface proximity trade-offs that matter most
In-app messaging removes the risk of sharing personal details upfront
For Zillow
Increases session depth by adding a social engagement layer to a search-only product
New monetisation: premium verified profiles, background checks, move-in concierge
Improves retention — Zillow becomes the complete rental platform, not just discovery
SF as a high-intent launch market before national rollout
No equivalent feature on Apartments.com, Trulia, or Craigslist
Success Metrics

How we would measure success

Metric 01 — Activation
% of SF rental searches with RoomMatch enabled
Feature adoption rate among the SF target cohort during active rental search sessions.
Metric 02 — Outcome
Successful roommate matches per month
A match is mutual connection plus at least one in-app message exchange. SF launch target: 500 in Month 1.
Metric 03 — Feature Adoption
% of match detail views with calculator used
Tracks whether the split rent calculator is a genuine decision-making tool, not a passive feature.
Metric 04 — Retention
Retention rate: RoomMatch users vs. non-users
Do users who engage with RoomMatch continue using Zillow after finding a roommate?
Metric 05 — Growth
% increase in new SF users via RoomMatch
Tracks net-new users who were previously using Craigslist or Facebook for SF roommate search.
North Star Metric
Users who save a listing and achieve a RoomMatch connection in the same session
The single metric proving the feature delivers its core value — property and roommate found in one session, without leaving Zillow.
Reflection

What the analysis reveals

Framing this proposal around San Francisco changes the nature of the argument. In a generic US rental market, shared living is a preference. In San Francisco, it is a financial necessity for most renters under 30. That distinction makes the split rent calculator a core part of the decision workflow, and the BART and Caltrain commute tags directly relevant rather than decorative.

San Francisco also makes sense as a launch market because the user density is high, the problem is acute, and the cohort is tech-literate — meaning adoption friction is lower than it would be in a mid-tier market. A successful SF launch would generate the data needed to refine the matching algorithm before national rollout.

"In San Francisco, the question is never whether you need a roommate. The question is how you find one you can trust, at a rent you can both afford, near the train you both need. That is a product problem — and Zillow is the right platform to solve it."
